Hi and welcome.

Here you’ll find a growing collection of useful scripts and notes to set up an open source based pipeline for Digital Cinema authoring. Read Mystery Meat to learn where the beef is.

To establish an open platform for digital cinema production and distribution is the general idea of the Digital Cinema Initiative and their specification. Much like the 35mm platform (specified in 1916) which has had a lot of breath.

The DCI had realized that in order to create a platform good for decades to come they needed to adhere to tried and tested standards. As a result you can satisfy the requirements for DCDM/DCP mastering and production with a set of almost ubiquitous open source tools. Which is the laudable kind of Good Thing geeks feel irresistibly attracted to, like bears to the honey pot.

A basic workflow to create DCPs includes

  1. Prepare the source material (Create an image sequence consisting of TIFF/DPX/Cineon files, wrap audio in Broadcast WAV container)
  2. Linearize RGB
  3. Transform color from source color to XYZ (see this nice visual CIE XYZ primer)
  4. Chromatic adaptation to the targeted white point (need to understand the problem better, see White Gamut)
  5. Encode with DCI spec’d gamma (2.6)
  6. Encode to JPEG2000 codestream files (The JPEG 2000 Profiles 3 and 4, used here, are lossy, btw)
  7. Collect assets (images, audio) in MXF containers/streams
  8. Create the basic infrastructure of a DCP (composition playlist, packing list, assetmap, volindex)

See Open source tools for a digital cinema pipeline for commandline examples.

The required information and knowledge is somewhat buried in man-pages, specs and SMPTE papers. Post-production facilities in the business are, understandably and yet regrettably, not exactly eager to share experience and practices. However there is no reason DCDM/DCP production should remain more complicated and opaque than, say, the creation of DVDs or BluRay media. That is the point of the platform.

Feedback, insights, code etc. very welcomed and much appreciated.
Wolfgang Woehl
